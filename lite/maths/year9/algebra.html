Algebraic Techniques

Master algebraic expressions, equations, and learn to solve linear equations and inequalities.

Year 9 New Content

Learning Objectives

After completing this topic, you should be able to:

  • Simplify algebraic expressions by collecting like terms
  • Expand and factorize algebraic expressions
  • Solve linear equations with variables on both sides
  • Understand and solve linear inequalities
  • Solve word problems using algebraic techniques
  • Graph linear equations on the coordinate plane

Introduction to Algebraic Techniques

Algebra is the branch of mathematics that uses symbols (usually letters) to represent numbers and quantities in formulas and equations. It allows us to solve problems by working with these symbols rather than specific numbers.

In Year 9, we build on your previous algebraic knowledge and develop more advanced techniques, including:

  • Working with more complex expressions
  • Solving equations with variables on both sides
  • Understanding and solving inequalities
  • Representing linear relationships graphically

These skills are essential for many real-world applications, from calculating distances and costs to analyzing data and making predictions.

Key Concept: Algebra allows us to work with unknown quantities and discover relationships between different variables.

Simplifying Algebraic Expressions

An algebraic expression is a combination of variables, numbers, and operations. Simplifying expressions makes them easier to work with.

Collecting Like Terms

"Like terms" are terms that contain the same variables raised to the same powers. We can add or subtract like terms to simplify an expression.

3x + 4y + 2x - y = (3x + 2x) + (4y - y) = 5x + 3y
Example 1: Simplifying by Collecting Like Terms

Simplify: 2a + 5b - 3a + 2b + 4

Solution:

2a + 5b - 3a + 2b + 4
= (2a - 3a) + (5b + 2b) + 4
= -1a + 7b + 4
= -a + 7b + 4

Expanding Expressions

Expanding involves removing brackets by multiplying each term inside the bracket by the term outside.

3(2x + 4) = 3 × 2x + 3 × 4 = 6x + 12
Example 2: Expanding Expressions

Expand: 5(3x - 2y + 1)

Solution:

5(3x - 2y + 1)
= 5 × 3x - 5 × 2y + 5 × 1
= 15x - 10y + 5
Common Mistake: When expanding expressions like 2(x + 3), some students only multiply the first term by the number outside the bracket: 2(x + 3) ≠ 2x + 3. Remember to multiply every term inside the bracket: 2(x + 3) = 2x + 6.

Factorizing Expressions

Factorizing is the reverse of expanding. It involves finding the common factors in an expression and writing it in the form of a product.

6x + 9 = 3(2x + 3)

To factorize an expression:

  1. Find the highest common factor (HCF) of all terms
  2. Divide each term by the HCF
  3. Write the expression as HCF × (result of step 2)
Example 3: Factorizing Expressions

Factorize: 12x² - 18x

Solution:

  1. Find the HCF: 12x² = 12 × x² and 18x = 18 × x. The HCF is 6x.
  2. Divide each term by the HCF:
    12x² ÷ 6x = 2x
    18x ÷ 6x = 3
  3. Write the expression in factorized form:
    12x² - 18x = 6x(2x - 3)

Solving Linear Equations

A linear equation is an equation where the variable has a power of 1. In Year 9, we learn to solve equations with variables on both sides.

Key Concept: When solving equations, what you do to one side, you must do to the other side to keep the equation balanced.

Steps for Solving Linear Equations

  1. Expand any brackets
  2. Collect like terms on each side
  3. Get all terms with the variable to one side and constants to the other
  4. Solve for the variable
Example 4: Solving a Linear Equation

Solve: 3(2x - 1) = 4x + 7

Solution:

3(2x - 1) = 4x + 7
6x - 3 = 4x + 7
6x - 4x = 7 + 3
2x = 10
x = 5

Check: Substitute x = 5 into the original equation:

3(2 × 5 - 1) = 4 × 5 + 7
3(10 - 1) = 20 + 7
3 × 9 = 27
27 = 27 ✓

Linear Inequalities

Inequalities compare expressions using the symbols <, >, ≤, or ≥ instead of equals (=).

Solving Linear Inequalities

The process for solving inequalities is similar to equations, with one important difference:

Important Rule: When multiplying or dividing both sides of an inequality by a negative number, you must reverse the inequality sign.

Symbol Meaning Example
< Less than x < 5
> Greater than x > 7
≤ Less than or equal to x ≤ 3
≥ Greater than or equal to x ≥ 10
Example 5: Solving a Linear Inequality

Solve: 2x + 5 ≤ 4x - 3

Solution:

2x + 5 ≤ 4x - 3
2x - 4x ≤ -3 - 5
-2x ≤ -8
-2x ÷ (-2) ≥ -8 ÷ (-2)
x ≥ 4

Note: We reversed the inequality sign when dividing by -2.

Answer: The solution is x ≥ 4, which means all values of x that are greater than or equal to 4.
